Pronoun Usage

We already talked about pronoun/antecedent agreement, but you must also know when to use which pronoun case (such as I versus me).

  • You must use nominative pronouns (such as I, he, and they) for subjects and predicate nominatives (for example, Jacob and I are studying for our math test).
  • You must use objective pronouns (such as me, her, and them) for direct objects, indirect objects, and objects of prepositions. (Will you eat lunch with Rudy and me?)
  • Most students have trouble deciding when to use who and when to use whom.
    Who (nominative case) is for subjects and predicate nominatives.
    Whom (objective case) is for direct objects, indirect objects, and objects of prepositions.

Possessive pronouns NEVER have apostrophes. A pronoun with an apostrophe is either a contraction (it’s=it + is) or NOT a real word at all. For example, its’ is not a word.Avoid the use of reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, etc.) Also, remember that hisself, theirselves, and themselves are not words!
