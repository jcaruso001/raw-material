Reducing Fractions

Reducing and simplifying fractions will require you to be able to identify the common factors be- tween the numerator and the denominator. This is where the divisibility rules will come into play. You will be looking for factors that can divide both the numerator and the denominator. You can do this either by writing out the prime factorization of both the numerator and the denominator or just by examination. Consider the problem of simplifying the fraction 420/630. You could simply write out the prime factorization of 420 and 630, then cancel any common factors. After some work you would arrive at the correct answer.

A second, and often quicker, approach will be simply to look for common factors by inspection. We can easily see that both 420 and 630 are divisible by 10. This leaves us with 42/63. Both 42 and 63 are divisible by 3 (the sum of the digits is 6 and 9, respectively), so we are left with 14/21. Removing the final common factor of 7 will leave the correct answer of 2/3.

You will probably encounter one or more improper fractions on the test. An improper fraction is one in which the numerator is greater than the denominator. Answers for the multiple-choice questions will typically be written as mixed numbers. Mixed numbers represent an improper fraction as a whole number and a fractional component. For example, 7/3 is an improper fraction. Three (3) divides into 7 twice with a remainder of 1. We could thus write 7/3 as the mixed number 21/3 .
