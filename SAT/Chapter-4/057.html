The Cartesian Grid

Every real point (x, y) has a place on this grid. For instance, the point A(3, 1) can be found by counting over on the x-axis three places to the right of the origin (0, 0) and then counting one place up. The point B(2, –1) can be found by count over to the right two places on the x-axis and then down 1 and point C(–2, –3) can be found by counting over two places to the left on the x-axis and then down 3.

Note: Always remember: moving up on a grid means y is becoming more positive, while moving right on a grid means x is becoming more positive


The Cartesian grid can be broken up into four quadrants with respect to the x-axis and y-axis. The signs of the x- and y- coordinates change depending on the quadrant in which the point lies. The quadrants are labeled from I to IV in a counterclockwise direction. In Quadrant I (top right), both x and y are positive. In Quadrant II (top left), y is positive whereas x is negative. In Quadrant III (bottom left), the values of both x and y are negative. Finally, in Quadrant IV (bottom right), x is positive while y is negative. The importance of this concept will become more apparent later when reflecting points or lines is discussed, since the signs in Quadrants I and III are exact opposites (the same is true for Quadrants II and IV).
