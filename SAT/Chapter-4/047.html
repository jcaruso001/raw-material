Angles are most often described by the points on the lines that intersect to form the angle or by the point of intersection itself. For instance, in the diagram below, angle ABC could be described as either ∠ABC or ∠x.


A line forms an angle of 180°. If that line is cut by another line, it divides the 180° into two pieces that together add up to 180° (∠x + ∠y = 180°). Two angles that when added together make 180° are called supplementary angles (∠x and ∠y are supplementary to each other).


When two lines intersect, they form four angles, represented above by the letters A, B, C, and D.
∠A and ∠B together form a straight line, so they add up to 180°. The same is true for ∠C and ∠D, ∠A and ∠D, or ∠B and ∠C. Since there are 180° above the line (∠A + ∠B), there are also 180° below the line (∠C + ∠D). Therefore, the sum of the four angles is 360°. ∠A and ∠C are opposite from each other and always equal to each other, as are ∠B and ∠D. These are known as vertical angles. When two lines meet so that 90° angles are formed, the lines are said to be perpendicular to each other. The 90° angle is called a right angle. A right angle is represented by a little box at the point of intersection of the two lines. Perpendicular lines are denoted by the symbol ⊥.


Two angles whose sum is 90° are said to be complementary. For instance, in

Based on what you’ve learned about vertical angles, ∠A = ∠C and ∠B = ∠D. Similarly for angles E, F, G, and H. Since the same transversal cuts line l2, the four angles ∠E, ∠F, ∠G, and ∠H are in the same places as ∠A, ∠B, ∠C, and ∠D respectively. These are called corresponding angles and each angle formed around the bottom line is equal to its corresponding angle formed around the top line.
