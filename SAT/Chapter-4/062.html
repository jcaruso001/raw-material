Radians

For trigonometry, oftentimes radians are used instead of degrees. Radians are the amount of circumference between the endpoints of the given angle for a circle with a radius of 1. A circle with a radius of 1 has a circumference of 2π. Consider a 180° angle. In a circle, a 180° angle has its endpoints at either end of the diameter. We can use the relationship between 180° and π to convert between degrees and radians when needed. Let’s convert 60° to radians. All we have to do is multiply by π/180

Now let’s convert π/4 radians to degrees. All we have to do is multiply by 180/π so that π will cancel out.

On the test, the most common angles used are the angles that can be found in the 30-60-90 and 45-45-90 triangles. This means that we can use our special triangles to easily find the trig values of the angles 30, 45, and 60. The radian measures for these angles are π/6, π/4, and π/3.

Example: Solve 2sin(x) −1= 0

We solve this like we would a normal equation by isolating sin(x).

2sin(x) −1= 0
2sin(x) = 1
sin(x) = 1/2

Looking at our 30-60-90 triangle we can see that sin(30) = 1/2
Thus, x = 30 or π/6.
